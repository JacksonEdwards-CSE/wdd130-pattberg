Minnesota

Idaho contains a diverse landscape shaped by volcanic eruptions, ancient ecosystems, and thousands of years of human history. The locations highlighted here represent some of the most unique geological and cultural sites in the state.

Itasca State Park

Itasca State Park has a wonderful story of love and preservation of nature. This park is where the headwaters, or source, of the mighty Mississippi is located. The river that shaped America, starts as a simple spring in a simple area. Before this location was protected, loggers were moving quickly through the forest to meet the demands of Americas expansion. Jacob V. Brower originally came to the area to settle a dispute about the location of the source of the Mississippi but left with a drive to protect the land from the loggers. Before the turn of the century, Brower was able to get state lawmakers to legally protect the land, making Itasca State Park the second oldest state park in the United States.

Interstate State Park

Interstate State Park is in Taylors Falls, MN, right on the border of MN and WI. This area was heavily flooded during the retreat of the last ice age. There is evidence of two erosion events. The first was when an ice dam broke and flooded water down the kettle river, eventually dumping into the St. Croix River. This washed away much of the surface layer in and around the river. The second drainage event caused the deep gorge that the St. Croix River resides in today and created the famous potholes. These potholes were caused by eddies within the floodwater. These eddies had such force that they essentially drilled these potholes with sand and gravel, and once large enough, stones that polished the inside of the potholes. This park has the largest grouping of these potholes in the world.

Blue Mounds State Park

Blue Mound State Park is a relatively small and simple park, but it stands out among the rolling prairies. This rock formation looks out of place, as native grasslands surround it. These rocks were originally formed by ancient rivers that deposited sand here. This sand was turned into sandstone as the weight of the river sediments continued to increase. Over the millions of years of heat and chemical reaction, the sandstone turned into quartzite, which is what we see today. During the last ice age, the glaciers slowly pushed this sandstone and quartzite together. This slow movement forced the rock into the cliffs seen today. Much of the land surrounding the park is of the same composition, just hidden under a layer of surface sediment and grasses.
